Understanding Manulex_infra

Consistency and Frequency of the grapho-phonological associations : The ambiguity of phonological coding from orthographic input, and the ambiguity of spelling from phonological input, are generally estimated by consistency index. In alphabetic scripts, consistency of the grapheme-phoneme (G-Ph) and phoneme-grapheme (Ph-G) associations is a critical factor in learning to read and write. The term "grapheme" is used here to refer to letter or letter groups that match a phoneme. Note that French includes several multi-letter graphemes such as "ou", "an", "un", "in", "eu", "ch", and "gn". The G-Ph consistency index is equal to the frequency at which a particular grapheme-phoneme mapping occurs divided by the total frequency of the grapheme, no matter how it is pronounced. The consistency of a G-Ph association thus reflects the probability of associating a particular phoneme with a given grapheme. For example, the G-Ph consistency index of the association "ch"->/S/ (as in the word "chat" /Sa/) is obtained by dividing the frequency of occurrence of the "ch"->/S/ association by the frequency of the grapheme "ch", irrespective of its pronunciation (including /S/, but also /k/ for example, as in "choral" /koRal/). The G-Ph consistency index is then multiplied by 100. Its maximal value is 100. Similarly, the Ph-G consistency index is equal to the frequency at which a particular phoneme-grapheme mapping occurs, divided by the total frequency of the phoneme multiplied by 100, no matter how the phoneme is spelled.

Consistency can differ greatly depending on the serial position of the grapheme or phoneme in the word. In particular, due to the derivational morphology of French, word endings are often silent, so spelling is less transparent. Thus, to better characterize the orthography-phonology mappings of French, frequency and consistency were computed as a function of the relative serial position: initial (first grapheme/phoneme), final (last grapheme/phoneme), and intermediate (graphemes/phonemes in the middle of words). Manulex_infra also provides two types of mean consistency and frequency: one lexical and the other textual. The lexical frequency (i.e. Count by-type) reflects the number of different words in the database that include a G-Ph and Ph-G correspondences, whereby each word is only counted once. Text frequency (i.e. Count by-token) reflects the number of words (different or identical) in the texts that include a correspondence of interest, whereby a word is counted as often as it appears in the corpus. So, frequency and consistency values of the G-Ph and Ph-G mappings are independent of the frequency of occurrence of words in the texts (rare or frequent words) in the by-type statistics, while the frequency and consistency values are weighted by the frequency of word occurrence in the by-token statistics.

Graphemic segmentation of French words is generally not problematic, although segmentation choices had to be made in some cases. As far as possible, the main principle was to segment the orthographic chains so that each segmented substring corresponds to a single phoneme. Our choices were governed by a second important principle according to which segmentation must highlight inconsistencies in the pronunciation/spelling of the words.

The presence of silent letters at the end of many words leads to different approaches for the analysis of G-Ph associations and the analysis of Ph-G associations. In the reading direction, G-Ph mappings involving silent graphemes can be described as the association between a grapheme and a silent 'phoneme' (e.g., 'd' in 'lourd', 'p' in 'loup', the gender inflection 'e' in 'amie', the number inflection 's' in 'tables'). For example, the final G-Ph association of the word 'nid' ending with a silent 'd' is strongly consistent because the final 'd' is rarely pronounced in French words. Conversely, the final 'd' in the word 'sud' is pronounced, and the consistency of the mapping between the grapheme 'd' and the phoneme /d/ is low. The presence of silent letters at the end of words must be considered differently when analyzing phoneme-to-grapheme associations (Ph-G; writing direction) because the graphemes are silent (e.g., /fulaR/ for the word "foulard" ending with the silent letter "d"). The solution adopted in Manulex-Infra v.2 is to group the final silent letters with the last pronounced grapheme (last phoneme). For example, in the writing direction, the final of the word "renard" is coded /R/-rd, the word "terre" /R/-rre, and the word "gare" /R/-re (the pronunciation of "renard" is thus compatible with misspellings such as "renarre", or "renare"). The coding principle is similar when the last phoneme is a vowel; "jamais" is coded /E/-ais, and "lait" /E/-ait.

Rime context. Grapho-phonological associatiations for word endings are also considered in the larger context of the phonological rime. These complementary analyses also address the observation that, in spelling production, orthographic choices are partly determined by the rime context. In this case, a silent grapheme such as 'd' in the word 'renard' is no longer integrated into the Ph-G association /R/-'rd' but into the final rime of the word, /aR/-'ard'. The phonological rime, as considered in version 2 of Manulex-infra, corresponds to the last vowel of the word, different from a schwa, the eventual semi-vowels which precede it, and the eventual semi-vowels, consonants or schwa which follow it. For example "ion-/jɔ͂/" in "nation", "illard-/jaR/" in "billiard". The semi-vowel that precedes the vowel is considered as part of the rime because spelling variability depends on whether the semi-vowel is taken into account or not. For example, for nouns, the final /ɔ͂/ ('on') can be spelled in many ways (on, ond, om, ont, onc, omb, ong), whereas in /jɔ͂/, the 'on' is not followed by silent consonants (ion, yon, illon in nation, rayon, bouillon). As with for the G-Ph and Ph-G associations, both consistency and frequency of the rime associations must be considered when evaluating word spelling difficulties. A highly consistent but rare association is a source of difficulties.To determine how difficult a word is to read or spell, consider its consistency not only on the final rime, but also on the G-Ph or Ph-G associations. Words can be highly consistent on rimes but inconsistent on G-Ph or Ph-G associations.

Analyses including or not the inflected (gender, number) and conjugated forms of verbs. The grapho-phonological relations (consistency and frequency G-Ph, Ph-G, rhyme) are analyzed either by including all the orthographic forms encountered in the textbooks, or by taking into account only the orthographic forms that correspond to the associated lemma (or lexeme). This second analysis allows a description of the grapho-phonological associations by excluding the variations related to gender (masculine, feminine) and number (singular, plural) inflections, as well as to verbal inflections. Note that words that appear in textbooks only in the inflected form are not taken into account in the second analysis since the simple form is not encountered by the child.

Other variables coded in Manulex_Infra v.2 The Manulex_Infra v.2 database provides additional information on lexical entries (a more detailed description is given under the 'download' tab):
• Least consistent or least frequent G-Ph or Ph-G association of the word (the association is provided along with the consistency or frequency values)
• Orthographic, phonological, graphemic, and syllabic length of the word
• Textbook word frequency at grade 1, grade 2, and grade 1 to grade 5 according to the Manulex database
• Syllabic segmentation of the phonological code, G-Ph and Ph-G segmentations, phonological rime (and its orthographic counterpart)
• Orthographic neighborhood according to the 'n-count' index or the 'Levenshtein distance' index. The n-count index counts the number of words that can be generated from a target word by substituting a single letter by another letter (e.g., 'rire' has as orthographic neighbors 'lire', 'rare', and 'rime'). The higher the value, the denser the orthographic neighborhood. The Levenhstein distance (OLD20) corresponds to the average number of orthographic changes to be made to a word (letter substitution, letter transposition, letter deletion or addition) in order to generate its 20 closest orthographic neighbors. The lower the value, the denser the orthographic neighborhood.
• Number of non-homograph homophones (e.g., port-porc-pore) for adjectives and nouns.
• Average frequency of bigrams
